# Gap: Can a familiar household object narrate years of human conflict in a way that feels honest rather than sentimental?

- **Domain:** Narrative voice
- **Type:** reaction
- **Status:** converging
- **Origin:** DATA-TOY / utility / house-biography / first-person-diary
- **Closure condition:** A human reaction to a short first-person object monologue that distinguishes honest restraint from sentimentality.

## Sessions

### 2026-07-10 · playmate
- **Scenario / mad-lib:** The user chose a household object, its age, and one repeated event it had witnessed; a short first-person monologue was written from that object’s perspective.
- **User inputs:** Object: floor lamp. Age: 12 years. Witnessed event: many arguments.
- **Artifacts:** One short monologue tested whether restrained physical detail and withheld explanation could carry emotional weight without tipping into melodrama.
- **Steer / outcome:** The user judged the monologue honest, not too sentimental.
- **Mined:** Verified: A household-object narrator can carry accumulated conflict without feeling sentimental when it stays concrete, observes the room rather than diagnosing the people, and lets repeated physical details imply the history. The emotional line landed because the object remembered atmosphere and behavior, not because it explained motives.
- **Left open:** How far the object’s voice can become more specific or more personal before restraint gives way to sentimentality.
