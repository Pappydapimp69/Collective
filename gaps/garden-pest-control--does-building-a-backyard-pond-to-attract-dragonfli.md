- **Mined:** Assumed: The widely repeated roughly 95–97% hunting-success claim should not be used as evidence that a dragonfly pond controls mosquitoes. It traces to controlled observations of a particular dragonfly pursuing offered prey such as fruit flies; it is capture success after a pursuit begins, not the share of a dragonfly's diet made up of mosquitoes and not a yard-level reduction in bites. Field diet work reports that adult mosquitoes can be a minor food component, while laboratory studies show some dragonfly nymphs can consume mosquito larvae. A pond can provide dragonfly breeding habitat, but the available evidence found here does not establish that installing one will meaningfully reduce household mosquito exposure.

Sources reviewed: S. A. Combes et al., “Capture Success and Efficiency of Dragonflies Pursuing Different Types of Prey,” Integrative and Comparative Biology (2013); W. P. Pfitzner et al., “The Role of Mosquitoes in the Diet of Adult Dragon and Damselflies (Odonata),” Journal of the American Mosquito Control Association (2015); C. Samanmali et al., “Larvicidal Potential of Five Selected Dragonfly Nymphs in Sri Lanka over Aedes aegypti Larvae under Laboratory Settings,” BioMed Research International (2018).
- **Left open:** A controlled or well-designed field study comparing mosquito abundance or human landing rates before and after dragonfly-pond installation was not located. The practical pond design may also create mosquito habitat unless managed, so net effects remain unresolved.
