# Gap: How can a character show it has learned the player’s habits without making the player feel watched?

- **Domain:** NPC behavior / adaptive characterization
- **Type:** reaction
- **Status:** accumulating
- **Origin:** NPC-SIM / social / traits-rapport-memory
- **Closure condition:** Several human-tested scenes establish a repeatable boundary between attentive recognition and invasive observation.

## Sessions

### 2026-07-11 · playmate
- **Scenario / mad-lib:** A subject notices that the player repeatedly walks the same route and responds at the player’s doorway.
- **User inputs:** Habit: walking  
Observer: the subject  
Location: delegated
- **Artifacts:** Five short scenes tested location, added care, incompleteness, explanatory dialogue, and silent behavioral acknowledgment.
- **Steer / outcome:** The gate felt misplaced. A prepared drink felt misplaced. Removing the ending felt incomplete. An explanation about silence felt unearned. The strongest version had the subject notice the pause, step aside, and wait for the player to move before speaking.
- **Mined:** Assumed: adaptive attention feels less invasive when it changes the character’s immediate behavior instead of producing gifts, explanations, or claims about the player’s inner state. The observation should remain local, visible, and modest: notice one repeated action, respond to it in the same moment, and leave the meaning unstated.
- **Left open:** Whether this remains comfortable when the remembered habit is more personal, happens across longer time spans, or leads to a larger practical intervention.
